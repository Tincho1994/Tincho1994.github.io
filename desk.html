Flat Packed Inspired Desk

Summer 2019

Overview

When I moved out of my tiny first apartment in San Francisco I finally had the space to build the big desk I always wanted. I wanted something I could use as a computer work station and a work bench for projects. I decided on an L desk with a section exposed on 3 sides, allowing for a walk around work area. Being a renter, I also wanted to capture the flat pack functionality I'd seen in Floyd furniture which allowed for easy moving. At the time I had just gotten access to one of Shaper Tools’ Shaper Origin (a handheld CNC) which enabled me to take a page out of Open Desk’s book and build entirely from plywood sheets and have precision capability in my cuts and joinery.

Process

I designed the legs in two halves to have cross braces slot into the legs. I reinforced the halves in shear by adding hidden dowels with blind holes. For the desk surface, alignment features were cut underneath to mate with the legs themselves. Using the Origin also allowed me to work in splines and stress relieving radii into the design.

Before starting on the actual cutting I did a good deal of prototyping and testing. I cut the joinery geometry out to test my clearances/ tolerances and get an idea for the offset I’d need for press fit and slip fits. I ended up settling on 0.03” oversizing for a slip fit and sizing to nominal for press fits. During my test cuts I also realized my feed speed and spindle speed needed optimization for the plywood. I tested both variables at different levels and looked at chip size and color of the sawdust to determine my optimal parameters.

The corner of the L turned out to be the most challenging feature. I wanted to maximize space underneath the desk so I opted for single supports on the corner legs. The corner legs however weren’t constrained well enough to withstand the moment applied by loading the inner corner. This led to deflection that warranted a few braces to attach the legs to the surface and improve the torsional resistance.

To finish I sanded it down to 400 grit and coated it in boiled linseed oil to seal. In the end, even with the brackets, I was able to assemble it with relative ease and flat pack it, keeping true to the original vision.
