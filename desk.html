Flat Pack Inspired Desk

Summer - Fall 2017
Master's of Engineering Project
Ithaca, NY

When I moved out of my tiny first apartment in San Francisco I suddenly had a lot more empty space to fill. The one thing I’ve always fantasized about in room decor is a nice big desk. I wanted something I could use as a computer work station and a work bench for projects. I decided an L desk with a section exposed on 3 sides, allowing for a walk around work area. At the time I had just gotten access to one of Shaper Tools’ Shaper Origin handheld CNC which enabled me to take a page out of Open Desk’s book and build entirely from plywood sheets.

I designed the legs in at least two halves to hog out the appropriate interior shape to make the overhangs for the cross braces to latch onto with hook features. Doing it in several pieces also allowed for hidden dowels to add some more rigidity. For the desk surface, alignment features were cut underneath to mate with the legs themselves. Using the Origin also allowed me to work in some more aesthetically pleasing splines and stress relieving radii into the design.

Before starting on the actual cutting I did a good deal of prototyping and testing. I cut the joinery geometry out to test my assumed clearances and get an idea for the offset I’d need for press fit and slip fits, settling on 0.03” oversizing for a slip fit and sizing to nominal for press fits. During my test cuts I also realized my feed speed and spindle speed needed optimization for the plywood. My chip size was much too small and the bit kept burning the wood. I tested both variables at different levels and looked at chip size and color of the sawdust to determine my optimal speeds.

When building, the corner turned out to be the most challenging feature. I wanted to maximize space underneath the desk so opted for single supports on the corner legs. Assuming everything was rigidly connected, the table should be adequately supported. The corner legs however weren’t constrained well enough to withstand the moment of applying weight to the inner corner, causing them to deflect slightly, warranting a few braces to attach the legs to the top surface and improve the torsional resistance.

To finish I sanded it down to 400 grit and coated it in boiled linseed oil to seal. In the end, even with the brackets, I was able to assemble it with relative ease and flat pack it, keeping true to the original vision.
