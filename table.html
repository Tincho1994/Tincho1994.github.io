Collapsible Table with Twist-On Legs

Summer 2020

Previous Next

Overview

This project was meant to serve as a proof of concept for an idea a designer friend and I had for collapsible furniture. The concept centered on being able to quickly flat pack wood furniture to store under couches, beds, etc. and quickly assemble when needed. A sitting area I built in my room was severely lacking a surface to hold my early afternoon coffee so I set off to trial this concept on a low coffe table that also happened to make a great picnic table to take to Dolores Park.

Design Process

The crux of the concept lies in the removable table legs. For this I wanted something that would be able to attach and remove without the need of external tools or a complicated latching mechanism. I settled on a twist to lock concept and set off from there.

When designing the leg key and the base slot, I wanted to be sure to allow for enough radial rotation to ensure the entire key arm was supported under the overhang of the slot feature while leaving room for a hard stop wall that would stop the rotation. I also wanted to make sure the locking action had a haptic click and also locked the key in the slot after rotating. Early ideas used a spring loaded ball and plunger but to keep things from getting too bulky I decided to have a spherical bump on the slot overhang that then locked into a deboss on the leg key. The fact that the overhang was cantilever would allow for enough deflection to allow the part to be clicked into and out of place by hand.

In order to design for manufacturing on a 3D printer I designed the slot base with an overhang to be printed in two parts. The leg key coud be printed in a single direction. The base slot parts are also designed such that they'd be fairly simple to machine on a lathe or CNC in case we wanted to trial metal parts. The leg key itself would need to be split into a few pieces or redesigned but also would be fairly simple to manufacture.

With the slot being printed in two parts I needed a means to join the components. Ideally, I'd have had enough room to mate the two parts using the same holes the screws that bound the base t the table would use. There wasn't enough room without increasing the base footprint or profile so I opted for bonding the two pieces with epoxy. The epoxy interface however would be partially in shear so I also added a small undercut in the main base that not only doubled as a glue overflow channel but would create a mechanical interlock between the two pieces once bonded.

Since the legs would be removable I needed to add some components to store them away. I designed some brackets that would hold the legs flat under the table. I sized them to be press fits so the legs would snap in and out with a push.

Prototyping

The main concept that needed some trial an error was the locking mechanism. I played around with different degrees of interference between the spherical potrusion an the leg key that would rotate underneath it until the boss fell into the leg key's slot. I was able to land on something that felt secure once locked and provided a satisfying click without providing too much resistance and deflection of the overhang.

In terms of part assembly prtotyping, I was able to carry over nominal offsets from other projects to adjust my part fit based on my printer's capability. I found the fit between the two base parts to be snug and lock in place just fine for testing, even without the aid of glue. Once bonded together with epoxy the bond was strong and the undercut channel worked great as a glue overflow channel.

Final Touches

For the table material I went with some Birch plywood and pine dowels I had handy. I also added a red oak trim around the table sides to hide the plywood layers. After sanding the whole thing down I stained it with some boiled linseed oil.

After assembly the table worked great. Although the printed material has it's limitations with regard to how likely the table could survive a drop or tumble, its held up well enough so far. I'd love to improve on it in the future and make it more robust because the convenience of being able to dissasemble and stash it under my bed or haul it off to the park has made it one of my favorite pieces.
